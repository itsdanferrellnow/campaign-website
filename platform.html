Dan's Platform

Asheville is too expensive, and I'll work make the city more affordable for everyone.

Making Asheville Affordable

I need to tell you a story.

In the winter of 2004, I lived outside of Chicago. I was living paycheck to paycheck, getting paid slightly above minimum wage, just scraping by like so many families do right now. I lost my job because I got sick for an extended period of time. I lost my housing because I lost my job. And I ended up homeless.

I slept wherever I could find for six months, walked three miles in below-freezing temperatures to work, and had a few friends who were able to support me in little ways. I eventually found enough good fortune to find a car to sleep in, allowing me to save a thousand dollars to rent an apartment (back when you could rent an apartment for $500/mo).

It wasn’t because I worked harder than any other person who has lost their home; it wasn’t because I did something that a lot of other families haven’t tried. It was simply because I just got lucky enough, long enough, to get back on my feet. Enough things went right for me for long enough that I was able to find some savings again.

I understand how hard it is here for people to get by in Asheville. I’ve been in the same places that many working class families in Asheville have been. I’ve felt how everything just squeezes in on you from all sides, and one wrong step can mean the difference between staying in your home or living on the streets.

What happens if the economy struggles under Donald Trump and the Republican-controlled federal government? What happens to the 54% of North Carolinians who are currently living paycheck to paycheck if they lose their jobs because the economy crashes? How many of our neighbors will no longer have a safe place to exist?

We need to plan for a future in the face of the problems of the present, and we can do that together.

After Hurricane Helene, we worked together to mend and make whole what could be repaired. We found housing for our neighbors; we made space at our table to feed each other. In times of chaos and struggle, like now, what stops us from doing that again?

This is a really personal story to me, and why I believe in the project of making Asheville affordable. If we want to prepare for an uncertain future, we need to actually prepare. Please read the other points for more specific policies I'll work towards.

Housing in Asheville is neither affordable nor accessible. We need to build housing that is accessible for everyone.

  • Mayor Esther Manheimer has been in office since 2009, and has been an integral part of developing the Affordable Housing Plan by the city [1], but given how out of control housing prices are, maybe it's time for a bold new plan.
  • Stop carving out exceptions on affordable housing that benefit developers rather than our neighbors.
  • Require every new residential development to have 15% of units[2] to be affordable for first-year teachers in Asheville.
  • Encourage and develop community partners for non-profit and co-operative housing developments in Asheville.
  • Ramp up access and knowledge about eviction and foreclosure assistance to our neighbors.
  • Encourage Housing First as a strategy to help our unhoused neighbors move into stability. This is more cost-effective than any other method [3].

Footnotes:

  • [1] - City of Asheville's Affordable Housing Plan
  • [2] - Davidson, NC's policy on affordable housing
  • [3] - Systematic Research Review Finds Benefits of Housing First Programs in U.S. Outweigh Costs

The city has tools to enforce under-utilized code requirements on bad landlords.

  • Too many rental units in Asheville are unsafe and don't meet updated code requirements for safety.
  • When our neighbors have complaints, often times nothing is done to remediate those problems.
  • I'll work to create a department for handling these cases with the power of imposing significant fines on repeat offenders.
  • For the chronic and egrigious violators who refuse to comply with code, I'll introduce ordinances to use the court system to seize the property and create non-profit housing from it.

Better Food Security

After the hurricane, the question before every one of us became: where were we going to get our next meal when we didn’t have power, water, or access to stores?

But the truth is, every day we all navigate our need for food. For some, it’s easy. Maybe even joyful. But because of the deeply inequitable way wealth is distributed in our community, for others getting enough to eat is nearly impossible. And it simply shouldn’t be that way. Every single one of us deserves access to healthy, safe food.

In a world where food safety inspectors aren’t checking to see if our food is safe, how do we know what we’re getting at the commercial grocery stores is safe anymore? When the federal government intentionally stops paying out SNAP benefits that go to 28,000 people in Buncombe County, it’s a crisis. It’s the same sort of crisis we experienced after Hurricane Helene, and we can respond to it as a city, as neighbors, together to help make sure kids and families don’t go hungry.

After the hurricane, when our community’s food needs were the greatest, Ingles, our neighborhood Corporate Grocery Store, blockaded their stores with police. And there’s two problems here that really should be talked about here: the first is that in a time where we could use everyone’s help for taking care of our community, especially our civil servants like the police, they were busy guarding Ingles' profits. And the second is that Ingles’ response to our food crisis didn’t reflect a true partnership with the food needs of our community.

My values say that in times of crisis, we all help each other. In that moment, the City of Asheville, the Asheville Police, and Ingles did not do everything in their power to help.

We must build a more resilient way to provide food to our community. We need food distribution centered on access, not on profits. And we need it to support the livelihoods of our area farmers. I’m proposing building a public-good grocery center providing safe, clean food that’s accessible to everyone. It would host diapers and formula at cost, and safe, local produce, from local farmers paid fairly for their time and effort. This is something we could be proud to see our tax dollars support.

After the hurricane, we all fed each other with the clarity that every one of us deserves to eat. We can intentionally rebuild this notion again. We can use our resources together to make it so that everyone, no matter your financial situation, can always get clean, safe groceries and feed their families. If we want to prepare for an uncertain future, we need to actually prepare. Please read the other points for more specific policies I'll work towards.

Kids should not have to go home to their family not having food.

  • Kids should not go hungry. There's no circumstance, no reason, for kids to go hungry. It's not just a policy failure, but a moral failure, and an improper use of our many resources.
  • In the weeks after Helene, we managed to feed everyone, regardless of circumstances. No one went hungry, there was enough for everyone, but then it stopped. We accepted the reality that it's okay that some people go without food.
  • If the federal government fails to provide SNAP and WIC benefits, we know how to feed our neighbors.
