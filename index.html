Every Neighbor Deserves Better

This town is not just for business owners, tourists, and retirees. It is a home for every single one of us. And none of us can truly thrive while our neighbors struggle to meet their basic needs. As a city councilperson, I hope to make city council act in a way that truly reflects that you matter here, too.

What Asheville residents deserve from city council:

Keeping Asheville Affordable

The cost of living keeps going up, families get priced out of living here, and housing insecurity is growing every year. Everyone knows it's tough to live here, but it does not have to be that way, city council just has to act on what's actually important for our neighbors.

Better Food Security

In the aftermath of Helene, our neighbors worked together to help feed everyone in our community. It's all of our jobs, including city council, to make sure none of our neighbors go hungry when the federal government works to eliminate subsidies.

Healthy Communities

When our neighbors can't afford healthcare because of greed, that's a crisis. When our rivers are polluted by industry because of greed, that's a crisis. Let's work together to make our communities safer for all of our neighbors instead of just a few.

An Economy for Everyone

The point of having a good economy in Asheville is so that all of our neighbors get a chance to live good, safe lives. Let's work to harness what makes Asheville amazing for our neighbors and keep the tourism money in the city instead of flowing into the pockets of developers.

I’m Dan Ferrell, and I’m running for Asheville City Council to truly make Asheville a safe town for all of us.

We live in a different world than we did a few years ago, and I want our town’s policies to reflect our community’s real needs.

We live in a time when our federal government is moving to force their brand of hate and cruelty on not only the most marginalized of our country, but on everyone else, too.

But we learned during the Hurricane who we are: that we could take care of our neighbors, how every one of us deserved care; and that at the end of the day, there was enough for all of us. We don’t have to live in a world of scarcity; we just have to live in one where we get the right things to the people at the right time.

When all of our usual systems were down, we worked together to build something deeply human. We fed each other, we clothed each other, we helped our neighbors find housing and community. We took care of each other, and in that we grew stronger.

But we’re facing another crisis, a longer crisis. One that will require us to act now if we want to be prepared. I saw so many of you out in the streets in 2025 demanding that we fight back against the federal government. I want to be a voice for our community that is clear that we need to prepare for a future that looks radically different from our past. Preparing is our first step to resisting.

We can prepare for long-term oppression by ICE, we can prepare for the end of federal democracy, the advent of a so-called 'King'. We see the federal government trying to starve our neighbors by eliminating the SNAP benefits, but funding ballrooms. They disappear fathers, mothers, children, based on where they were born or simply on their perceived ethnicity. They criminalize LGBTQ+ people and take away their access to healthcare and identity. They imprison, attack, and kill Black and Brown people with increasingly unmasked hatred.

The federal government is making communities everywhere unsafe for so many, but we can build a place of resilience against that right here, right now. We can protect ourselves and our neighbors because we all deserve the safety to live our lives free from the chaos of state-funded terror.

Whether our future holds attacks from our own government or new climate-related catastrophes, there are ways we can increase our resilience and security as a town for the benefit of everyone, not just for the wealthiest. As a city counselor, I will act in a way that reflects the real danger that exists in front of us, emphasizing food and water security, as well as the housing needs of the majority.

I plan to explore ways to deepen neighborhood organizing, because as we saw after the hurricane, there are times when our neighbors are all we have. And on the political level, when neighbors come together to identify their own greatest needs, we can deepen our democracy by listening to every neighborhood, not just the loudest or wealthiest ones.

This town is not just for business owners, tourists, and retirees. It is a home for every single one of us. And none of us can truly thrive while our neighbors struggle to meet their basic needs. As a city councilperson, I hope to tip the scale to more deeply reflect that you matter here, too.
